# Overview

## Context

The client is a Brazilian e-commerce company selling nutritional supplements via WooCommerce. By late 2025, their data infrastructure had accumulated several years of unmanaged technical debt. The system had been built incrementally without engineering governance: pipelines were added as needed, schema constraints were never enforced, and no one had audited the data quality since the platform launched.

The company was engaged to rebuild the data platform and deliver an analytics layer on top of it. Before writing a single line of code, I ran a full technical audit to understand what was actually in the system, because touching a data warehouse without understanding its state is how you turn a data quality problem into a data loss incident.

## What the audit found

The audit revealed seven critical findings across the data warehouse and the analytics application.

**Bot attack contamination.** The customer table contained 62,466 fraudulent records, 68% of the total, generated by three waves of bot registrations over the lifetime of the platform. Every metric derived from customer data, including acquisition rates, segmentation, and RFV scores, was inflated by approximately 200%.

**No primary keys on 8 of 22 tables.** Without primary keys, duplicate records were inevitable and idempotent pipelines were impossible. Any UPSERT strategy would fail silently or create logical duplicates.

**26,921 orphan records in fato_vendas.** These were orders referencing customer IDs that did not exist in the customer table. The root cause was the absence of foreign key constraints: the pipeline had been inserting orders with WooCommerce customer IDs that were never synced to the warehouse, and PostgreSQL had no mechanism to reject them. The orphan rate was 49.7% of all orders.

**RLS disabled on 17 of 22 tables.** Customer PII, including 12,609 CPFs, was accessible via anonymous PostgreSQL key without any authentication. This was a direct violation of LGPD Art. 46.

**OAuth2 credentials stored in plaintext.** A table named `integracoes` stored Bling ERP OAuth2 tokens as plain text in the database. Anyone with read access to that table could compromise the ERP integration and, by extension, the company's fiscal operations.

**16 monetary fields typed as TEXT.** Financial calculations were being done on the client side, casting strings to numbers, which produced rounding errors and made server-side aggregations unreliable.

**Pipelines stopped.** WooCommerce sync had been down for 73 days. Bling sync for 40 days. Nobody had noticed because there was no alerting.

## Solution approach

I chose a Parallel Migration strategy, also known as the Strangler Fig pattern, rather than correcting the existing database in place. The in-place approach would have required taking services offline, running destructive migrations on live data, and hoping that the existing pipelines continued to work against a schema that was changing underneath them.

The parallel approach meant building a clean production environment from scratch, migrating only validated and corrected data, and keeping the legacy environment running for all active services until the new one was ready. The AI agent (conversational agent) and the N8N automation continued operating without interruption throughout the migration.

The project covered four areas: data warehouse reconstruction, application security and performance fixes, ETL pipeline infrastructure on AWS Lambda, and observability.
